[Chris Blattman says a third of emails make him
cringe](https://chrisblattman.com/2010/11/08/students-how-to-email-to-your-professor-employer-and-professional-peers/).
I would double that number at least, and it is not always students on the
sending end. Given that issue has been addressed in many places, I am writing
this specifically for my students (or those students that want to work with me)
as a primer on email pro fessionalism, but I am certainly borrowing ideas from
other similar posts around the internet. I owe special thanks to Chris Blattman,
Steven Miller, and for posting their own examples of these policies. Anything
similar here should . My hope is that students continue to implement these
suggestions after graduation so the post is written when that idea in mind.

### Use your school- or company-provided email

In an age where email accounts are inundated with spam, you are best served to
use the email provided to you by your university (in the case of students) or
your company (in the case of professionals). I will not respond to random email
addresses unless I immediate recognize the person. In the case of current
students I will *only* respond to official university addresses.

### Always have a salutations and a sign off

I am barely old enough to have received basic instructions in writing letters.
This was an important rule, and it applies no differently to emails. If you skip
either of these steps I am far less likely to respond helpfully---if I respond
at all. I want to know who you are and it is always nice to address the person
to whom you are sending a message. When it comes to addressing your professors,
most will appreciate you adding Professor XXXX or Dr. XXXX instead of "Ms." or
"Mr." or their first names. If you do not know their proper title, then take the
time to visit their department or personal webpage. I promise you they will
appreciate the effort.

### Write professionally

An email to your professor is not the time for corresponding in shorthand. The
rise of social media and texting has lent the world to shortcuts that should be
be relevant when writing an email. Do as you please in your private life, but if
you want to present yourself as a competent student then write as if you are
working on the final draft of a formal paper. You will always do better to err
on the side of formality in professional settings, and email is no different.

### Two examples

I am often asked to meet with students to discuss some topic from a class. I am
going to give two examples, one good and one bad:

#### Good example

> Dear Dr. Wimpy,
>
> My name is XXXX, and I am a student in your XXXX class. Would you be willing
> to meet to discuss the analyses in my paper? If so, is there a good time or
> should I just come by during office hours
>
> Thank you,
>
> XXXX

#### Bad example

> Hey when wus the test?

I hope you can tell the difference.

### Dr. Wimpy's email policies

I try to use email expeditiosly and efficiently. This means that

I am usually quick to respond to student e-mails during regular business hours.
However, student e-mails tend to do several things that try my patience. I have
a new policy, effective Fall 2019, that outlines why I will not respond to
certain e-mails students send. Multiple rationales follow. \_Note that some of
these may not apply specifically to this course.\_

1\. The student could answer the inquiry by reading the syllabus.

2\. The student provides an unsolicited reason for missing class (other than
exam days). I do not need to know the exact reason why you missed class.
Students with excusable absences are responsible for giving me a note that
documents the reason for a missed exam. Anything else is just extra information.

3\. The student wants to know what topics were missed during a missed/skipped
class. The answer is always "you missed what was on the syllabus."

4\. The student is not professional in the e-mail. Please consider writing
professional e-mails, it's a good habit that will pay off one day.

5\. The students wants to know something that is readily discernible from
Blackboard (e.g., "how many discussion posts do I have left?," "what is my
current grade?").

6\. The student is requesting an extension on an assignment for which the
syllabus already established the deadline. The answer is always "no."

7\. The student is ["grade
grubbing"]([https://www.math.uh.edu/\~tomforde/GradeGrubbing.html)](https://www.math.uh.edu/~tomforde/GradeGrubbing.html))
or asking to round up a grade outside of the rounding outlined in the grading
policy above. The answer is always "no."

8\. The student is asking for an extra credit opportunity, a request that
amounts to more grading for the professor. The answer is "no."
